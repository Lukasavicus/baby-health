Evidence-informed activity menu for children from birth to three years
Sources and scope
This report synthesizes caregiver-guided activities for children from birth up to age three, grounded in materials from SBP and WHO, with an emphasis on play, daily routines, and caregiver–child interaction. The core “activity streams” below come directly from: (a) WHO’s early childhood development guidance prioritizing responsive caregiving and early learning in the first three years; (b) WHO’s 24-hour movement guidance (active play, limiting restraint and screens, and sleep); and (c) SBP materials that translate these ideas into age-appropriate tips and concrete examples (including talking, reading, music, blocks, pretend play, and outdoor/nature contact). 

It is important to be explicit about what “comprehensive” can mean in this context. Neither SBP nor WHO attempts to publish an exhaustive list of every possible game or activity a child might do; instead, they provide evidence-informed recommendations and toolkits that focus on (1) responsive caregiving, (2) opportunities for early learning through interaction, (3) physical activity and healthy daily rhythms, and (4) safe, enriching environments (including the benefits of time outdoors). The activity library in this report is therefore designed as a comprehensive menu of practical options that operationalizes those recommended patterns into many concrete, do-at-home activity choices. 

Key primary documents used most heavily include the WHO ECD guideline summary and Q&A (for the “why” and “what”), the WHO under-5 movement guideline executive recommendations (for active play, restraint, screen time, and sleep), and SBP’s milestone-and-activity guide (“Cartilha”) plus SBP’s nature-oriented manual (for concrete play and routine ideas, including water/earth/plant contact and outdoor time). 

Foundational principles for selecting and using activities
Across WHO and SBP materials, the “best” activities for ages 0–3 are less about buying specific toys and more about how adults engage with children: with warmth, responsiveness, language, and safe opportunities to explore. WHO explicitly recommends responsive caregiving and early learning activities with caregivers during the first three years of life. 

The principles below help you choose and adapt any activity (books, blocks, box-drop games, water play, textures) so it becomes developmentally meaningful:

Lead with responsiveness (“follow the child”): notice the child’s signals, respond in a timely/appropriate way, and treat back-and-forth interaction as the “engine” of learning. 
Pair play with communication: talk, sing, and name what the child is doing/seeing; imitate and expand their sounds/words; use eye contact and facial expression. This is repeatedly emphasized in caregiver toolkits and SBP’s age-based tips. 
Embed learning into daily care (feeding, bathing, dressing, bedtime): WHO notes responsive caregiving and early learning can be incorporated into everyday routines, not only “special play time.” 
Protect daily rhythms: WHO’s movement guideline treats health as the balance of active play, limited restraint/sitting, minimal screen time, and adequate sleep across the 24-hour day. 
Limit screens and prioritize human interaction: WHO does not recommend screen time for infants; for 1-year-olds, sedentary screen time is not recommended; for 2-year-olds and older it should be no more than 1 hour/day (less is better). SBP messaging is consistent in discouraging screens under age 2 and setting tight limits thereafter. 
Use nature and outdoors as an “activity multiplier”: SBP recommends daily opportunities (at least about an hour) to play/learn in contact with nature and highlights environments with plants, earth, and water—especially valuing free play, movement, and sensory experiences in early childhood. 
Comprehensive activity library organized by developmental target
WHO defines early childhood development as spanning cognitive, physical, language, socioemotional, and motor development (among other aspects), and its guidance highlights that the first years—especially up to age three—are highly sensitive to responsive caregiving and early learning opportunities. 

To make the library usable, each category below lists activity types plus multiple concrete examples. Many examples come directly from Care for Child Development Counselling Cards and Developmental milestones guide (Cartilha de Desenvolvimento – 2 meses a 5 anos) and are consistent with WHO’s movement and ECD guidance. 

Responsive caregiving, affection, and secure attachment (birth–3 years)
Examples (use these throughout the day, not only during “play time”): skin-to-skin contact and gentle holding; soothing touch/stroking; holding the child close during feeding while looking and talking/singing; making “turn-taking” interactions by responding to sounds and copying gestures; praising effort and desired behaviors; helping the child calm down with your presence, a quiet safe spot, and simple calming strategies (for older toddlers). 

Language and communication (birth–3 years)
Examples: talk face-to-face at the child’s eye level; narrate what you’re doing (“Now we wash hands…”); copy baby sounds and then add a new sound; call the child’s name and wait for a response; name objects the child points to and repeat/expand their attempts (“ball” → “yes, a big ball!”); teach gestures like “bye-bye”; ask simple questions and respond to attempts to talk; teach songs and rhymes with gestures; give simple 2–3 step directions as the child approaches age three (“go to your room, get shoes, bring them”). 

Books, pictures, and early literacy (birth–3 years)
Examples: look at colorful photos/pictures together (“read” pictures even before the child talks); point and label what is in pictures; ask simple picture questions (“what’s happening?” / “what do you think comes next?”); talk about nature, pictures, and things the child sees; build a calm bedtime routine that includes reading 1–2 books; use books and stories as a preferred quiet-time activity instead of passive screen viewing (WHO explicitly encourages reading/storytelling during sedentary time). 

Sensory and perceptual exploration (visual, touch, sound, movement, taste) (birth–3 years)
Examples: slowly move a colorful object left/right/up/down for the baby to track with eyes; give safe objects to handle, bang, and drop (pots and spoons are explicitly suggested); provide varied safe textures (for example, SBP encourages selecting toys with richer texture/weight/aroma over uniform plastic in some contexts); explore sound-making (rattle, drum, banging a spoon on a pot); safe mouthing exploration for babies with objects that are not sharp/hot and not small enough to choke; offer a variety of food textures appropriate to age and feeding stage; sensory experiences outdoors (plants, earth, water) with supervision. 

Fine motor and hand–eye coordination (mostly 6 months–3 years, with earlier foundations)
Examples: grasp-and-release play (grabbing safe toys, then dropping); container play (put in/take out) using large safe objects and a container; stacking and nesting objects; clothespin-style clipping into a container; blocks—stacking and knocking down; shape sorting; simple puzzles; art exploration with thick crayons, smearing paint, coloring lines/shapes; practice self-feeding skills with spoon and open cup (messy but important); “helper” tasks that use hands (put items in a basket, carry napkins to the table). 

Gross motor, coordination, and active play (birth–3 years)
Examples: interactive floor-based play; tummy time while awake for infants (WHO specifies at least 30 minutes/day for non-mobile infants, spread through the day); rolling to reach toys; kicking and reaching games; crawling/chasing games; walking, running, climbing stairs with support as appropriate; ball play (roll, chase, kick); dancing/moving to music; “ready–set–go” movement games (including playground movement like gentle swinging); outdoor active play as a daily default. WHO recommends at least 180 minutes/day of physical activity for ages 1–2, and for ages 3–4 at least 180 minutes including 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity. 

Cognitive skills and problem-solving (object permanence, cause–effect, early math) (mostly 9 months–3 years)
Examples: hide-and-find games (hide a favorite toy under cloth/box; peek-a-boo); name-and-find (ask the child to bring a named object); toys that show cause-and-effect (press a button and something happens); sorting by color/shape; count-and-name everyday items (body parts, steps, objects); “what is this?” questions to spark thinking; help the child try solutions to small problems with your support (WHO’s early learning emphasis includes learning through interaction with people/objects in the environment). 

Social–emotional development and “emotional intelligence” (birth–3 years)
Examples: label the child’s feelings and give words for emotions; model gentle touch and simple rules; praise positive behavior more than you correct; teach empathy by commenting on others’ emotions (“that child looks sad”); offer simple choices (“red shirt or blue shirt?”); expect tantrums and respond calmly (distraction or brief ignoring can be appropriate, while keeping the child safe); teach calming strategies (deep breath, hug a comfort object, go to a quiet safe spot); encourage interaction with other children and support early sharing/turn-taking. 

Nature-based play, water/earth/plant exploration, and outdoor routines (especially valuable from infancy onward)
Examples: consistent outdoor walks and moments in natural areas for babies and caregivers; contact with plants, earth, and water; free play outdoors emphasizing movement and sensory experience; weather-flexible outdoor play with safe clothing (including rain play—jumping in puddles, making small “dams,” floating leaf boats); playground time; picnics and outdoor family time; choosing outdoor play as a strategy to reduce “digital intoxication” and protect family connection. SBP’s nature manual explicitly recommends daily access to opportunities to play/learn/convive with nature and highlights early childhood as a period where free play, movement, and sensory experiences outdoors are central. 

Age windows and what to prioritize
Both WHO and SBP emphasize that development is continuous, but some activity types become especially useful (or feasible) in certain windows. Below is a practical prioritization map that aligns WHO’s age bands (infants; 1–2 years; 3 years within the 3–4 band) with SBP and Care for Child Development examples. 

Infancy through the first year: prioritize responsive caregiving, floor play, sensory exploration, and constant “language bath.” This includes eye contact and talking/singing, back-and-forth sound imitation, visual tracking with colorful objects, safe grasping/mouthing exploration, and supervised tummy time and free movement on the floor. WHO discourages screen time in this period and encourages reading/storytelling when calm and sedentary. 

From the first birthday through the second year: prioritize high-volume active play (WHO: at least 180 minutes/day), container play (put in/take out), stacking/nesting, naming/pointing, simple questions, picture-book talk, and early pretend. Continue to minimize restraint and treat reading/storytelling as the preferred quiet activity. WHO does not recommend sedentary screen time for 1-year-olds; for 2-year-olds it should be ≤1 hour/day. 

From the second birthday up to the third: prioritize peer exposure and social play, early “rules,” emotional language, simple chores, richer pretend play, puzzles/sorting, and structured-but-playful language building (expanding phrases, 2–3 step directions). SBP’s tips for this period strongly emphasize routines, early literacy practices, emotion coaching, and limiting screens. 

At age three (approaching WHO’s 3–4 band): keep 180 minutes/day of activity with purposeful energetic play built in (WHO specifies at least 60 minutes moderate-to-vigorous activity in the 3–4 band), combine daily reading with more “why/what/where” conversation, and add simple counting/comparison games. Screen time should remain at or below 1 hour/day (less is better), consistent across WHO and SBP messaging. 

Safety, inclusion, and practical setup
Safety is not separate from development—WHO’s nurturing-care framing treats safety/security as a core precondition for learning and thriving, and WHO’s movement guideline explicitly warns against long periods restrained and excessive sedentary screen time. 

For infants and toddlers, several safety points recur in SBP/WHO materials and matter for many of the activities above:

Supervision and positioning: SBP advises supervised tummy time while awake and emphasizes safe sleep arrangements (firm surface, no loose items), which matters when mixing play and rest routines. 
Choking prevention in play and feeding: SBP explicitly recommends allowing babies to explore with the mouth using safe items and warns against objects small enough to choke; it also advises discussing solid-food introduction and choking-risk foods with a clinician. 
Limit time “restrained” in seats/strollers: WHO recommends infants and young children not be restrained for more than 1 hour at a time; SBP similarly advises avoiding spending too long in swings/strollers/“little seats.” 
Water play demands constant adult attention: SBP promotes contact with water in nature contexts, but any water play should be done with close supervision (especially for ages under three), with shallow setups and an adult within arm’s reach. 
Outdoor play: dress for safe exploration: SBP explicitly encourages outdoor play across weather conditions with appropriate protection (hat/sun protection, layered clothing, rain gear) so outdoor time remains feasible and safe. 
Inclusion and special situations: WHO’s movement guideline is written for healthy under-5s and notes that caregivers of children with disabilities or medical conditions may need additional advice from a health professional; WHO’s ECD Q&A also states the guidance is relevant for children with disabilities or illness, but adaptation and added supports may be needed. 

Primary references used
Improving early childhood development: WHO guideline — recommends responsive caregiving and early learning activities during the first three years; defines core concepts and integration into daily routines. 

WHO guidelines on physical activity, sedentary behaviour and sleep for children under 5 years of age — provides the 24-hour recommendations, including “tummy time,” activity minutes by age, restraint limits, and screen-time guidance. 

Benefits of Nature in the Development of Children and Adolescents (Benefícios da Natureza no Desenvolvimento de Crianças e Adolescentes) — SBP recommendations on daily outdoor/nature contact (plants/earth/water), free play, and practical outdoor examples (including rain/puddle play). 

Developmental milestones guide (Cartilha de Desenvolvimento – 2 meses a 5 anos) — SBP-distributed age-based tips (“help your child learn and grow”) that include concrete play, language, routine, and self-regulation activities up through age three. 

SBP public guidance on screen time by age — reinforces avoiding screens under age two and limiting to about one hour/day for ages two to five.
